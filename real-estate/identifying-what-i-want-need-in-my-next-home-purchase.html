Identifying What I Want/Need In My Next Home Purchase

A house is an important investment and one of the largest purchases most people will ever make.

Before buying a home, potential buyers need to consider exactly what they want—and, more importantly, what they need—in their next home purchase.

Alice Kuder, a Realtor for Prudential Northwest Realty in Seattle, suggests that homebuyers should start with their bigger, broader needs and then get into more specific wants from there.

“It’s important to go from what they think they must have, to what they prefer, to what they don’t care for or don’t care about,” Kuder says.

If you are trying to decide which factors to prioritize in your next home purchase, consider these seven steps:

1. Decide where you want to live. Consider what type of location would work best for you and your family. Do you want to stay in your current town? Do you want a place in the heart of the city? Would you prefer a quiet neighborhood with good schools?

2. Know what you can afford. According to the federal Department of Housing and Urban Development’s (HUD) website, no more than about one-third of your income should be spent on housing. The website offers a mortgage calculator that allows you to see how much you can afford to pay per month.

When calculating your housing expenses, don’t forget to consider factors like utility costs and home upkeep.

3. Decide what type of housing you want. Single family? Multifamily? A condo? The kind of living space you need will depend, in part, on your family situation, according to the National Association of Realtors.

Lori Fischer, creative director of Rethink Home Interiors, helps sellers make their homes appealing to potential buyers. Fischer says it’s important for buyers to focus on “flow of space,” or how they think they are going to live in a particular space, before making a purchase.

Along with deciding on the type of housing they prefer, buyers should also consider the number of bedrooms and bathrooms they need, Kuder says.

4. Decide what condition you want your potential home to be in when you purchase it. Some buyers want a fixer-upper; others want nothing to do with home repair, Kuder says.

“It’s important to know how much work they want to do,” she says. “Do they only want to do cosmetics? Do they want to move walls around? Does it need to be in move-in condition?”

5. Make a wish list. Assess what you like and dislike about where you currently live, Kuder suggests.

“Sometimes we’re so used to [the current home] that you don’t even realize what spaces you’re using more than others,” she says.

HUD offers a printable homebuyer’s checklist to help buyers “get the juices flowing” as they decide what they want in a their new space, says HUD spokesman Brian Sullivan. That list includes factors like how much renovation you’d be willing to do, how close to public transportation you’d like to be and what style of house appeals to you most.

“A lot of these things [on the checklist] are no-brainers, you’d think, but if you’ve never bought a home before, you may not be thinking about all these things,” he says.

6. Prioritize. Not everything on your wish list will be absolutely necessary, Kuder says. For example, many buyers think they need bathtubs, she says, but in reality, “you ask when they last took a bath and they won’t even be able to remember.”

The same goes for large yards and garage space, she says.

Use your checklist to help you separate the needs from the wants.

7. Be prepared to change your mind. “It’s important to allow for evolutions as [prospective homebuyers] go looking because what they think they need in the beginning and ‘must have’ often will change as they go around and start looking at things,” Kuder says.

It’s OK to update your wish list as you go, she says, because you’ll probably be living in your new home for a while.

“I put resale value way down on the bottom of a priority list,” Kuder says, “because what’s important is you, right now, and how much you’re going to enjoy your space.”
